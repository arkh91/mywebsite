Results for Lab6: A few things of note from students who have completed the lab.
1. If you run bump on otter you will get about 3 x the running time as on the cubs. Don't know why. (Thanks, Cole.)
2. If you use deques (double-ended queues -- also defined in STL) instead of vectors for the list of upper-cover lex numbers, you will cut your running time in about half. (Thanks, Cole.)
3. If you sort the elements in the deque of upper-cover-lex numbers before you compare, your running time on pos.25000.treduc will be 30 x higher. But if you do your pushes to the correct end of the deque in the right order, the values will be sorted so you don't have to. (Thanks, Tony.)

Here are some sample inputs and outputs:

$ ./mmopt 8 10 11 12 3 2 9 8 7
((x(x(xx)))((xx)x)) 952

$ ./mmopt 7 7 4 2 9 3 3 8
((xx)(((xx)x)x)) 288

$ ./mmopt 8 7 4 11 2 9 3 3 8
((x(xx))(((xx)x)x)) 376

$ ./mmopt 8 7 6 2 3 4 5 3 7
((xx)(((xx)x)x)x)) 318

$ ./mmopt 10 10 11 12 5 3 16 17 19 4 8
((x(x(xx)))((((xx)x)x)x)) 3255


Note that, by changing "<" to ">", we can find the optimally bad rather than good way of parsing the matrix chain. For example, the same matrix chain below can take either 608 scalar multiplications, or 45,680 scalar multiplications, depending on how you parse the matrix chain!

$ ./mmopt 15 2 20 2 20 2 20 2 20 2 2 20 2 20 2 20
(((xx)((xx)((xx)((xx)(x((xx)(xx)))))))x) 608

$ ./mmbad 15 2 20 2 20 2 20 2 20 2 2 20 2 20 2 20
(x((xx)((xx)((xx)((x(xx))((xx)(xx))))))) 45680
